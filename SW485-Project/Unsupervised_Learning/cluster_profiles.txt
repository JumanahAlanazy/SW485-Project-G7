Clustering Profiles Summary:

Dataset: Indian Liver Patient Records
Methods: K-Means Clustering & Hierarchical Clustering



K-MEANS CLUSTERING:


Cluster Interpretation:

Cluster 0:
Patients with relatively normal liver enzyme levels and low bilirubin.

Cluster 1:
Patients with elevated bilirubin and abnormal liver enzyme levels, indicating possible liver dysfunction.

Cluster 2:
Patients with moderate or borderline biomarker levels, potentially representing an early-risk group.


Key Findings:

- The Silhouette Score (0.188) and Davies-Bouldin Index (1.65) indicate weak cluster separation and overlapping boundaries.
- BCubed Precision is high (0.96), meaning patients grouped together tend to share similar characteristics.
- BCubed Recall is low (0.30), indicating clusters do not fully capture disease distribution.
- This suggests liver disease progression is continuous rather than discrete.

Conclusion:
K-Means produces compact and efficient clusters but struggles to fully separate medical conditions due to overlapping patient profiles.



HIERARCHICAL CLUSTERING:


Cluster Interpretation:

Cluster 1:
Severe cases with high bilirubin and elevated liver enzymes (strong liver inflammation).

Cluster 3:
Moderate cases with intermediate enzyme levels (possible early-stage risk group).

Cluster 2:
Normal or healthy patients with low enzyme levels and bilirubin.


Key Findings:

- Silhouette Score (0.1587) and Davies-Bouldin Index (1.7764) indicate weak separation and overlapping clusters.
- BCubed Precision (~0.61) shows moderate correctness of grouping.
- BCubed Recall (~0.51) shows better coverage of disease distribution than K-Means.
- Clusters align more with severity progression than strict classification.

Conclusion:
Hierarchical Clustering provides more interpretable, medically meaningful groupings but is sensitive to outliers and still shows overlap in patient profiles.



OVERALL:


- K-Means:
  → Better internal clustering quality
  → More compact clusters
  → Higher precision but lower recall

- Hierarchical Clustering:
  → Better clinical interpretability
  → Captures disease progression more effectively
  → Higher recall but slightly weaker structure metrics

Final Insight:
Both methods confirm that liver disease data is continuous rather than naturally separable. K-Means is more practical for system deployment, while Hierarchical Clustering is better for interpretation and analysis.
